Hi Elijah, 

I just spent some time going through your essays on "When Domain-Specific
Models Win" and "Towards Dedicated Model Foundries", as well as Jesse's
investment thesis "Variant 4: Autonomy".

The purpose of this note is to share our point of view on the five open
questions you raised in your [post about "Towards Dedicated Model
Foundries"](https://x.com/PossibltyResult/status/2077475857845608766).

Since we are in complete agreement with your view on verifiers and RLVR, and on
the market opportunity for SLMs (Specialised Language Models), I won't preach
to the choir. Where our experience at Tacit gives us a different angle, we've
laid it out below against each question.


> How much enterprise demand needs to be aggregated to overcome the billions of
> dollars in CapEx gaps between challengers and today’s frontier labs? Is this
> even feasible?

The assumption you are making here is that in order to deliver value to
enterprise, there needs to be an equivalent CapEx for a Modal Founday to
develop or post-train a model with similar capabilities.

We believe this is already feasible without matching frontier-lab CapEx. Based
on our experience working with large enterprise customers, the immediate
bottleneck to enterprise adoption is **model reliability**, not reasoning
capability or access to more compute.

A main part of the reliability challenge is hallucination, which is actually
getting worse as the models getting bigger, as shown by the [AA-Omniscience
Hallucination Rate
study](https://artificialanalysis.ai/evaluations/omniscience#aa-omniscience-hallucination-rate). [1]

Another aspect of this unreliability is how easy it is to pertube the
models' output through a manipulation to the input prompts. We have delivered
proofs where over one third of surgery triage outcome gets flipped via a few simple word
changes, where each case can cost an insurance company thousands to tens of
thousands of dollars. 

The insurance industry and the entire field of Actuarial Science is proud of
how they can "tame uncertainty" through applying probability theory and
statistics to assess risk and calculate premiums. It is our observation that 
the reliability challenge creates a cognitive dissonance that blocks the industry
from adopting LLM. 

We are already seeing repeated signals that today's LLM is already sufficient.
The enterprise market is ready for a generational transformation, which will
happen very incrementally. One workflow at a time. 

Our case study for New Zealand's Financial Markets Authority (FMA) is an
excellent example that shows that our Causal Inference Engine is the right
answer to solve the reliability problem for enterprises.


> Opportunities for domain-specific models to be competitive arise when
> reasoning is sufficiently idiosyncratic to that domain. Here domain-specific
> models can overcome the scale economies and convenience for users that
> general models have.

We agree about the reasoning capability is a critical piece for adoption, but
we'd argue that there are other capabilities that are equally important but
have been overlooked by the industry. In an enterprise deployment context,
where the LLMs are increasingly deployed alongside a human operator, ignoring
these capabilities is leaving a lot on the table.

A good example is behaviour.

The frontier models are all trained to "be helpful". This may be the right
behaviour for many use cases but can be wrong in others. For example, in a
patient in-take situation, it'd be a mistake to help **without** 
elicit and confirm the context of the case first. [2]

Today's frontier models already contain these information. But they are  
almost guaranteed to do the wrong thing because of this built-in behavior. 
In fact, we have established that the bigger the models are, the more likely
for them to jump to a conclusion despite being asked to seek context first. 

We have shown with our healthbench result that it is possible to train the
model to adopt a new default behavior with $40 USD training budget.
https://www.tacitintelligence.co/field-notes/trained-specialised-language-model-healthbench


> How does the tradeoff space between in-context and in-weights learning affect
> this thesis? For example, if in-context learning is sufficient for
> incorporating proprietary know-how into AI workflows for the vast majority of
> scenarios, selling tokens, rather than custom, post-trained models may be
> sufficient as well.

In-context learning is sufficient for occasional steering, but not for
continuous learning. We expect SLMs to learn in their weights from feedback and
call frontier models when they need broader capability.

Another axe that we find really exciting is Continuous Learning, which is only accessible
to SLMs. 

Given the training time and capex expenditure, in-context learning is acting as
**the** way to steer models, at least for the frontier ones. I remember that
Microsoft used to release software every year with quarterly security patches
shipped to subscribers. Today's Frontier Models feel like that. But what an
awful update cadence that is. SDFT/SDPO have shown how easy it is to
continuously train a model given textual feedback. 
https://self-distillation.github.io/user_interactions

Here is a short fun demo of a coding environment that is SDPO powered:
https://github.com/sdan/continualcode/blob/master/docs/continualcode.mp4 
Now, imagine this experience is replicated in every software you use.

Currently, most people interact with the frontier models directly. We actually
see a future where SLMs and frontier models co-exist in parallel but
individuals will increasingly interact with SLMs, which can "tool call" out to
frontier models when the situation calls for it.


[What does standardization look like for Post-Training-as-a-Service? Can it
even become sufficiently standardized as we’ve seen play out in chip design?]

We think the training process can be standardised even when each domain's
judgement cannot. Recursive Decomposition provides a common process: break tasks
into atomic facts, train against the richer signal, and continue decomposing the
high-variance parts.

> To date, there are two paths to winning for domain-specific models:
> - Compete on the cost of inference
> - Compete on quality of inference
> As is the case across any business, the extent to which a moat can be
> established is the extent to which it can persistently outperform, whether on
> cost or quality. 

There is a Jevons Paradox dynamic playing out here. 

In our framework, the cost and the quality are actually highly aligned. I 
mention the Recursive Decomposition process briefly in our call. Allow me expand
it a bit more. 

In a judgement-critical task like review surgery requests, the end result can
only be the result of 3 value: `REVIEW`, `PASS` or `REJECT`. So from RLVR's
perspective, the credit assignment is a massive headache. (I'd say borderline
impossible given how little training data available.)

By decomposing this task into hundreds of atomic facts, we observe that the
model's reliability improves and the RLVR training effectiveness improves
dramatically faster because of the rich high-dimensional training signal it
receives. We also found that this approach is very sample efficiency as you 
could imagine by now.

By recursively decomposing the task, we can continue decomposing the high
variance atomic facts until the whole system is not only performance well 
but also very reliable. 

What's unique about our approach is the inverse relationship between the
lowering of the inference cost and the improvement of the quality of the
inference. Particularly, we are keeping a close eye on optimising but for
continuous batching and throughput. Speculative decoding, hand-written CUDA
kernels, custom chips (or even FPGA) and separation of the Prefill and Decode
phase are all advances we've seen in lowering the inference cost. We predict
this trend to continue and we will benefit from its advancement without
compete in that area. 


[Can or will enterprise headcount evolve around the talent needed to push more
post-training responsibility to the edges?]

Yes, but enterprises need domain experts at the edge, not full frontier-model
teams. Their role is to turn judgement into feedback, evaluations and training
data while the foundry supplies the training infrastructure.

> Variant invests in technology that expands autonomy. We focus on ... that
> give users more agency through increased access, knowledge, and ownership.

One fact that is counter-intuitive to many people working in the AI field is
the idea that there is no single right answer to any given problem. In finance,
every trade is a proof where given the same market information, one person 
wants to sell while the other seems to be sure that it is the right time to
buy. In high performance sports, each elite player will be facing the same
situation and come up with drastically different solution that is only optimal
given their own training and physical faculty. 

You wrote about "Inaccessible Verifiers". You mention the wet labs and 
regulatory access as examples of this. We believe that the "ultimate" 
Inaccessible Verifier is in everyone's head. It's the personal preferences, 
value judgement and unique background that makes it impossuble to build 
a "general purpose" AI that works out of the box for everyone.

To us at tacit, the right end goal is to create an environment where an
Peronsalised AI can be trained, deployed and improved at a personal level. In
fact, one of the earliest stated goal for tacit is to facilitate individual
flourishing. We want to see a future where the toil of doing the same thing
again and again gets replaced by the creativity spark unleashed by this vision
of Personalised AI.


> Unlike general reasoning models, the primary moat for domain-specific models
> is not scale economies. ... So as different domains vary in these factors, so
> do the moats of companies building within these domains, and, as a result, so
> does their ability to sustain an advantage on quality.
> ...
> To overcome the discrepancy, frontier labs have deployed strategies to
> internalize domain and professional expertise. For example, in spending
> massive amounts on hourly wages to expert marketplaces like Mercor

Mercor is fundamentally built on project and task-based training data
(specifically expert-labeled feedback, evaluations, and RLHF) rather than
simple skills-based data. Mercor acts as a pipeline between top AI labs (like
OpenAI and Anthropic) and human domain experts (doctors, lawyers, programmers).
Experts are not building "skills." Instead, they are evaluating model outputs,
grading answers, and creating rubrics based on real-world, highly specific
domain scenarios.

As we've discussed at length in our calls. Skills and task completion are two
very different things. One is a chef that can cook up a delicious meal with
whatever ingredients on hand. The other is a finished meal made to spec. The
world needs both but we are interested in the former. 

For any task, there are many orthogonal skills required. When the training data
focus on task completion, it's usually focused on a domain specific skill yet 
overlook skills that are crucial to perform the task. The behaviour change we 
mentioned earlier is a good example of that. 


[Can weights remain open if “dedicated model foundries” monetize proprietary
hosted services around post-training? Or do the weights need to also be
proprietary for the business to be defensible?]

Yes. The weights can remain open if the foundry's defensibility comes from the
training platform: architecture choices, hardware support, reliability and
malleability. Proprietary weights are one possible moat, not the only one.

> But what they [Dedicated Model Foundaries] gain in incentive alignment, they
> lose in a moat. If they don’t own the end models, or post-training data, they
> can’t establish the same compounding data advantages that vertical and
> frontier AI companies do. So where could the moat come from?

The foundaries' moat will come in as platform lock-in, which reflects on architecture
choices and hardware supports. 

As a data provider looks for foundaries to parter with, we have to compare them on
multiple dimensions, instead of only on price. Factors like **reliability** and
**malleability** are two things we are already looking at. (We have an essay coming
on this shortly.)

For example, Nemotron 3 uses a hybrid model of Memba+Transformer. Although this
gives their model speed advantages, great 1M context window support in a small
model, and the thinking traces are often 30% shorter than Qwen and Gemma
models, it's been an absolute pain to train the Memba layers. Further, during
inference time, if we run the same request eight times, it often fails once or twice. 

On the hardware side, we foresee labs will in-house many of the chip design
tasks in order to get the most of their training/inference investment. When
Microsoft released their MAI models, they also released the Micorosft Maia
chips which allows them to train and inference at FP4 precision. Same for
LongCat-Flash by meituan. They trained the model with a 100X larger embedding
layer and in order to both train and serve inference, they developed their own
ASIC based pods. 

Even we are doing post-training work, we are clear-eyed about this trend. Our
focus is on how to design the most effecient post-training pipeline to take
advantage of the high-dimensional training data that we have. We plan to keep
our training software choice neutral and open source so we can avoid platform
lock-in.
