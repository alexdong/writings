I just spent some time going through your essays on "When Domain-Specific
Models Win" and "Towards Dedicated Model Foundries", as well as Jesse's
investment thesis "Variant 4: Autonomy".

The purpose of this note is to share our point of view on the five open
questions you raised in your [post about "Towards Dedicated Model
Foundries"](https://x.com/PossibltyResult/status/2077475857845608766).

Since we are in complete agreement with your view on verifiers and RLVR, and on
the market opportunity for SLMs (Specialised Language Models), I won't preach
to the choir. Where our experience at Tacit gives us a different angle, we've
laid it out below against each question.


> How much enterprise demand needs to be aggregated to overcome the billions of
> dollars in CapEx gaps between challengers and today’s frontier labs? Is this
> even feasible?

The assumption you are making here is that in order to deliver value to
enterprise, there needs to be an equivalent CapEx for a Modal Founday to
develop or post-train a model with similar capabilities.

We believe this is already feasible without matching frontier-lab CapEx. Based
on our experience working with large enterprise customers, the immediate
bottleneck to enterprise adoption is **model reliability**, not reasoning
capability or access to more compute.

A main part of the reliability challenge is hallucination, which is actually
getting worse as the models getting bigger, as shown by the [AA-Omniscience
Hallucination Rate
study](https://artificialanalysis.ai/evaluations/omniscience#aa-omniscience-hallucination-rate). [1]

Another aspect of this unreliability is how easy it is to pertube the
models' output through a manipulation to the input prompts. We have delivered
proofs where over one third of surgery triage outcome gets flipped via a few simple word
changes, where each case can cost an insurance company thousands to tens of
thousands of dollars. 

The insurance industry and the entire field of Actuarial Science is proud of
how they can "tame uncertainty" through applying probability theory and
statistics to assess risk and calculate premiums. It is our observation that 
the reliability challenge creates a cognitive dissonance that blocks the industry
from adopting LLM. 

We are already seeing repeated signals that today's LLM is already sufficient.
The enterprise market is ready for a generational transformation, which will
happen very incrementally. One workflow at a time. 

Our case study for New Zealand's Financial Markets Authority (FMA) is an
excellent example that shows that our Causal Inference Engine is the right
answer to solve the reliability problem for enterprises.


> Opportunities for domain-specific models to be competitive arise when
> reasoning is sufficiently idiosyncratic to that domain. Here domain-specific
> models can overcome the scale economies and convenience for users that
> general models have.

We agree about the reasoning capability is a critical piece for adoption, but
we'd argue that there are other capabilities that are equally important but
have been overlooked by the industry. In an enterprise deployment context,
where the LLMs are increasingly deployed alongside a human operator, ignoring
these capabilities is leaving a lot on the table.

A good example is behaviour.

The frontier models are all trained to "be helpful". This may be the right
behaviour for many use cases but can be wrong in others. For example, in a
patient in-take situation, it'd be a mistake to help **without** 
elicit and confirm the context of the case first. [2]

Today's frontier models already contain these information. But they are  
almost guaranteed to do the wrong thing because of this built-in behavior. 
In fact, we have established that the bigger the models are, the more likely
for them to jump to a conclusion despite being asked to seek context first. 

We have shown with our healthbench result that it is possible to train the
model to adopt a new default behavior with $40 USD training budget.
https://www.tacitintelligence.co/field-notes/trained-specialised-language-model-healthbench


> How does the tradeoff space between in-context and in-weights learning affect
> this thesis? For example, if in-context learning is sufficient for
> incorporating proprietary know-how into AI workflows for the vast majority of
> scenarios, selling tokens, rather than custom, post-trained models may be
> sufficient as well.

In-context learning is essential for exploratory steering, but prone to 
hallucination and pertubation attacks as we've mentioned above. 
Empirically, we have found that in-weight learning is a far superior 
approach to encode the process from the in-context learning while delivering
a stable and reliable output in real-world production environment.

A flywheel-style process we are recommending to our customers is to capture the
experts' feedback as part of the in-context learning process. We then recommend
incorporating these into the continuous learning process as priviledged context
for [SDFT/SDPO](https://self-distillation.github.io/user_interactions) and other
on-policy training methods. Here is a short fun demo of a [coding environment
that is SDPO
powered](https://github.com/sdan/continualcode/blob/master/docs/continualcode.mp4),
imagine this experience is replicated in every software you use.


> What does standardization look like for Post-Training-as-a-Service? Can it
> even become sufficiently standardized as we’ve seen play out in chip design?

We believe there is a standardization story here that will increasinly be 
driven by the shape of data/training signal.

For general LLM computing, the standardisation will increasingly concentrate at
the API level. For example, HuggingFace's `trl` and Thinking Machine Lab's
`tinker` both offer PPO and CISPO as an API call. What differentiates one
offering to another is no longer the compute or algorithm, but the training
data and the judgement behind these data.

Once the post-training infrastructure is sufficiently commoditised, 
the value will naturally gravitate towards where it was created and where it
should rightly remain: the enterprises themselves. Tacit's strategic goal is to
become the infrastructure that enterprises use to capture and deliver 
the expert judgements.

As an interesting thought experiment, once we embrace this atomic unit of LLM
inference idea, we can significantly improve both the inference and
post-training performance through, literally, hardware engineering of AI ASICs
and specialised chips. In the FMA case, instead of post-training a general
purpose LLM, we only need to post-train 8 several order-of-magnitude smaller
Specialised Language Models (SLM) where the end result can only be the result
of 3 value: `REVIEW`, `PASS` or `REJECT`. A back-of-envelop calculation
actually suggests that this is reasonably doable for under $1M budget. [3]



> Can or will enterprise headcount evolve around the talent needed to push 
> more post-training responsibility to the edges?

Yes, with continuous learning, enterprises need domain experts operate at the
edge. Their role is to turn judgement into feedback while the foundry churns
out the models on a regular cadence. Instead of a model release cycle every 6-9
months, the updates to the Causal Inference Graph will be constant and backward
compatible so releases will be daily if not more often. 

One fact that is counter-intuitive to many people working in the AI field is
that there is no single right answer to many real-world problems. In the 
financial market, every trade is a proof that two people can reach opposite 
conclusion given the same piece of information.

You wrote about "Inaccessible Verifiers". You mention the wet labs and 
regulatory access as examples of this. We believe that the "ultimate" 
Inaccessible Verifier is in everyone's head. It's the personal preferences, 
value judgement and unique background that makes it impossuble to build 
a "general purpose" AI that works out of the box for everyone.


> Can weights remain open if “dedicated model foundries” monetize proprietary
> hosted services around post-training? Or do the weights need to also be
> proprietary for the business to be defensible?

Whether the weights produced by the dedicated model foundries remain open or
closed is likely to become irrelevant as enterprises adopt LLMs into their
workflows. 

Since the the post-training weight for an SLM is a compressed artefact of
the expert judgement encoded into the training dataset, the weight will be of 
very low value or even detrimental to others.

For example, vehicle insurance is a transactional, commoditized product driven
by statistical data (e.g., make, model, driving record) and governed by annual
renewals. In contrast, life insurance is a consultative, emotionally-driven
product requiring deep trust, personal disclosure, and long-term planning.

It would be a mistake to use weights trained for vehicle insurance's customer
support in the life insurance settings. Further, even within the same 
life insurance company, the weights for one salesperson will eventually be so
tied to that individual's personal identity, preference and disposition that it
is essentially counter-productive for someone else to use those weights.


[1]: The ever increasing importance of harness like Claude Code, Codex or OpenClaw 
shows that we need a layer of deterministic logic to constrain LLMs output,
giving them a chance to retry and recover. Harness helps by giving some 
grounding via code and verifiers but it's not a fix. 

[2]: For example, it is usually a good idea, when treating mild
sprains/strains, use pracetamol for pain or anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
like ibuprofen to reduce pain and swelling. However, if the client has a
history of stomach ulcers, in third trimester of pregnancy or had a severe
allergic reaction to NSAIDs medicines, they should NOT take the medicine. If an
inadequate in-take interview misses any of the key criteria, a simple case
might balloon into a far more complicated and costly headache.

[3]: I worked with Codex on some back of the envelop calculation for a Hybrid
LoRA-Capable ASIC design. 

* The base model weight is stored in hardwired RRAM/NVM (instead of VRAM/HBM)
* The LoRA adapters, multi-tenant scheduling, in on-chip configurable SRAM where 
  the back-propogation happens and the gradient changes will be stored
  (instead of the VRAM/HBM again as they are the industry's bottleneck.)
* There will be ultra-low latency (near-zero adapter overhead) for inference
* Very low energy consumption <10W (instead of 300W to 700W for a Nvidia chip).
* FPGA Emulation on high-end FPGAs like AMD-Xilinx Versal will cost about 
  3-6 Months for $200k to prove the hybrid design.
* A full production run of 250k will likely cost $15-50M with 1.5-2 years.
